The Three Columns Explained

Each vent type (Ridge, Box, Power, Solar, Turbine) has the same three fields. They exist because of how insurance actually pays for storm damage: insurance only covers replacing vents that are damaged — undamaged vents just get physically reinstalled onto the new roof at no material cost. Ridge Vent is the one exception, since the ridge vent strip is torn off and replaced brand-new on every reroof regardless of damage.

1
Existing

How many of this vent type are on the roof right now, before any work starts — matches the insurance adjuster's count of what's up there today.

2
Desired Existing

Undamaged units that will be physically unscrewed and reinstalled as-is on the new roof — no new material, no charge. This can never be more than Existing — the calculator automatically caps it there.

3
Desired New

Damaged units being replaced with a brand-new unit, plus any genuinely additional units beyond the original count if the homeowner wants more than they had. The math below is what decides which of those two situations you're actually in.

💡
The rule that actually matters As long as Desired Existing + Desired New adds up to no more than the original Existing count, the charge is $0 — it doesn't matter how many of those are "reinstalled" vs. "brand new due to damage," because insurance already covers getting back to the original count either way. You only get charged once the final total goes above the original Existing count — that's the part insurance never covered.
Your example — 5 Box Vents, 2 damaged, 3 fine to reinstall
Roof currently has 5 Box Vents.
3 are undamaged and get reinstalled as-is.
2 are damaged and get replaced brand-new (insurance-covered).
Existing5
Desired Existing (undamaged, reinstalled)3

The part people get confused on
How Credits Work — You're Not Charged Twice

Under the hood, the calculator does two price lookups per vent type, then subtracts them:

A
Price the final total

Cost of (Desired Existing + Desired New) units — i.e. everything that will be on the roof when the job's done.

B
Price the original Existing count

Cost of the Existing units, at that same per-unit rate. This is the "credit" — insurance already covers getting the roof back to its original vent count, whether that's achieved by reinstalling undamaged units or replacing damaged ones brand-new, so that count shouldn't be billed again here.

=
Charge = A − B

In plain terms: you only get charged once the final total goes above the original Existing count — that's the portion insurance never accounted for.

Worked example — adding beyond the original count (Box Vents, $16.50/ea + 8% tax = $17.82/ea)
Existing: 2 (say, both undamaged)
Desired Existing: 2 (both reinstalled)
Desired New: 1 (homeowner wants 1 more, beyond original count)
Final total (2 reinstalled + 1 new) → 3 × $17.82$53.46
Credit for original Existing → 2 × $17.82−$35.64
Ventilation upgrade charge$17.82

Notice the charge ($17.82) is exactly the cost of one new unit — this time it's a real charge, because the final total (3) is genuinely more than the original Existing count (2). Compare this to the "5 Box Vents, 2 damaged" example above, where the final total matched the original count and the charge was $0 — the difference is entirely about whether the total goes past the original count, not about which units are labeled "new."

Box, Power, Solar, Turbine only — not Ridge
Removing Vents Without Replacing Them Still Costs Something

If more vents come off than go back on (a net reduction), the credit math above can actually go negative — which would look like a refund. It isn't one: someone still has to physically deck over and patch each unused hole. So a flat $35 patch fee per net-removed unit gets added back on top for Box, Power, Solar, and Turbine (Ridge Vent doesn't get this fee).

Worked example — Box Vents: removing 2, replacing 0
Existing: 3
Desired Existing: 1
Desired New: 0
Final total (1 at prior level) → 1 × $17.82$17.82
Credit for original Existing → 3 × $17.82−$53.46
Raw credit math (would look like a refund)−$35.64
Net removed = 3 − 1 kept-level − 0 new = 2 units → 2 × $35 patch fee+$70.00
Ventilation upgrade charge$34.36
⚠️
Why this isn't a mistake Removing 2 net vents with no replacement still shows a real, positive charge ($34.36) instead of a credit — that's the patch/deck-over labor, and it's expected.
Power & Solar Power Vents only
Power & Solar Vents Work Differently — No Credit, Ever

Box, Turbine, and Ridge Vent follow the credit rule from the previous sections (charged only if the total goes above the original Existing count). Power Vents and Solar Power Vents don't follow that rule at all. They're treated as a premium, discretionary upgrade — so any brand-new unit is billed at full price, with no credit against the old one it's replacing, even if the final total doesn't exceed the original Existing count.

⚡
This is the opposite of the Box Vent "5 existing, 2 damaged" example earlier For Box Vents, replacing 2 damaged units out of 5 existing came out to $0 because insurance already covers getting back to the original count. For Power/Solar, that same shape (existing unit swapped for a brand-new one) is always a full charge — Desired Existing is still a free reinstall if you're keeping the physical old unit, but Desired New is never discounted against Existing.
Worked example — existing Solar Power Vent swapped for a brand-new one
Existing: 1
Desired Existing: 0
Desired New: 1
1 new unit ($438.00/ea + 8% tax)$473.04
New-install fee (Power/Solar only)+$50.00
Ventilation upgrade charge$523.04

Note there's no credit line at all here — unlike the Box Vent examples, the Existing count of 1 never gets subtracted. The $50 install fee (covering the extra electrical/mounting work these two vent types need) applies on top, any time Desired New is greater than 0.

Ridge Vent only
Ridge Vent Is Entered in Linear Feet, Priced in 4-Foot Bundles

Ridge Vent is the clearest example of why "Desired Existing" isn't about physically reusing old material — the ridge vent strip gets torn off and replaced as part of any reroof, there's nothing to reinstall. Existing/Desired Existing/Desired New here just track how much ridge venting footage is already covered by the base roof price versus how much is new added scope.

It's also the one exception to "counts" — all three fields are entered as linear feet (LF), not unit counts. Pricing then rounds up to the nearest 4 LF "stick" — so 21 LF is billed as 6 sticks (24 LF worth), not 5.25 sticks. This same rounding applies on the credit side too, so most of the time it works out cleanly, but a job with an odd LF count on the Existing side can round the credit up slightly as well — worth double-checking on unusual numbers.

Worked example — Ridge Vent ($10.25/LF + 8% tax, sold in 4 LF sticks)
Existing: 40 LF
Desired Existing: 40 LF
Desired New: 20 LF
Final total 60 LF → 15 sticks × $10.25 × 1.08$166.05
Credit: original 40 LF → 10 sticks × $10.25 × 1.08−$110.70
Ventilation upgrade charge$55.35
After the math
Where the Homeowner Charge Actually Comes From

Everything above produces the "Ventilation upgrade charge" line shown under the vent grid — that's the material/labor cost baseline after credits and fees, not what the homeowner necessarily pays.

🎛️
The slider/HO Charge box below it is a separate step Just like every other material in the calculator, you still set the actual amount charged to the homeowner using the markup slider or by typing a dollar amount directly. If the Ventilation upgrade charge comes out to $0.00 (for example, only removals with no net reduction), the box switches to letting you type any flat dollar amount you want to charge instead — same behavior as other $0-cost line items.
